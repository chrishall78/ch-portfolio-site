Designing the World's Most Expensive Hotel:

A special Presentation - December 5, 2015

Soon after Mauna Kea Beach Hotel opened in July 1965, Esquire magazine immediately named it one of the “three greatest hotels in the world” and the American Institute of Architects (AIA) presented the hotel an Honor Award citing “restrained detailing and fine spatial sequences.” A decade later, AIA placed Mauna Kea on its prestigious America's Favorite Architecture list. When all was said and done, venture capitalist Laurance S. Rockefeller's creation on Hawaii Island's Kohala Coast was the most expensive hotel ever built at the time, to the tune of $15 million, more than $113 million in today's dollars.

Leading architectural firm Skidmore, Owings & Merrill (SOM) was commissioned by Rockefeller to provide design options for the hotel. Nine schemes were presented from their East and West Coast offices. The story of how “Scheme 7” was selected is fascinating.

On Saturday, December 5 at 5pm, SOM San Francisco Senior Consulting Design Partner Craig Hartman will be on site at Mauna Kea Beach Hotel. In his complimentary one-hour talk story presentation, Hartman will share background on the scheme-that-almost-was, and what happened, on the head-to-head design war between their own offices, and how SOM designers Chuck Bassett and Mark Goldstein ultimately came through with the design for the mega-structure of 154 guestrooms that addressed all of Rockefeller's must haves and have-nots.

Don't miss this rare opportunity for insight and anecdotes about the challenges and rewards of designing the award-winning mid-century modern Mauna Kea Beach Hotel.

Hartman's talk story will include a display of archival images during the construction as well as a slide presentation. Reservations are not required.

conservation efforts

My Mauna Kea

The My Mauna Kea program unites guests and employees of Mauna Kea Beach Hotel in the restoration of the fragile native forest on the slopes of the hotel’s namesake Mauna Kea volcano – the tallest mountain on earth.

The critically endangered palila, a finch-billed species of Hawaiian honeycreeper, is found only on the southwestern slopes of Mauna Kea. Due to centuries of land transformation and deforestation, only 2,000 palila remain.

The Mauna Kea Beach Hotel has joined the Mauna Kea Forest Restoration Project and Hawaii Forest and Trail to create the My Mauna Kea stewardship program. This program provides unique opportunities for hotel guests and employees to support and participate in reforestation efforts to provide future palila habitat. Upcoming excursions: March 16 and April 6, 2015. Call 808-882-5707 for reservations.

To learn more about the My Mauna Kea program, download an informational brochure or visit Restore Mauna Kea.
